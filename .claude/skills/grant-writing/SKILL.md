# Grant Writing Skill

Secure non-dilutive funding. Grants are free money — write them like you're making a business case, not asking for charity.

---

## Before Starting

1. **Grant type** — SBIR/STTR, federal agency, state ag program, university, foundation?
2. **Stage** — Phase I (feasibility) or Phase II (development)?
3. **Amount** — What's the max award? What's being requested?
4. **Deadline** — How much time is there?
5. **Eligibility** — Does the applicant qualify? (Small business, US-based, nonprofit, etc.)

## SBIR/STTR Application Structure

### Phase I Core Sections

**1. Project Summary (1 page)**
- Problem significance — why it matters, who suffers, what it costs
- Innovation — what's novel about your approach
- Commercial potential — market size, customer evidence
- Team qualifications — why you can do this

**2. Project Description (15-25 pages depending on agency)**

#### Section A: Significance
- Current state of the problem (cite data, not opinion)
- Who is affected and how severely
- Economic, social, or scientific impact of solving it
- Why existing solutions are inadequate

#### Section B: Innovation
- What specifically is novel (vs. prior art)
- How your approach is different from the competition
- Any preliminary data or proof of concept
- Intellectual property position (patents, trade secrets)

#### Section C: Approach
- Specific aims with measurable milestones
- Technical approach for each aim
- Alternative approaches if primary fails
- Timeline (Gantt chart or milestone table)
- How you'll know if it worked (success criteria)

#### Section D: Team & Resources
- PI/co-PI qualifications (focus on relevant experience)
- Key personnel roles
- Facilities and equipment
- Subcontractors or consultants

**3. Commercialization Plan**
- Target market with size estimate
- Customer discovery evidence (interviews, LOIs, pilots)
- Business model (how you'll make money)
- Go-to-market strategy
- Path to Phase II and beyond

**4. Budget**
- Personnel (breakdown by role and % time)
- Fringe benefits
- Equipment (one-time purchases)
- Travel (conferences, customer visits)
- Subcontracts
- Other direct costs
- Indirect costs (F&A rate)

## Writing Principles

### Write for a program officer who isn't an expert in your field
Avoid jargon. If you must use a technical term, define it. Reviewers often span multiple disciplines.

### Lead with impact, not technology
"This will help ranchers catch disease 3 days earlier, saving $400/head" beats "Our ML model achieves 91.6% classification accuracy."

### Cite everything
Every claim about market size, problem severity, or technical feasibility needs a citation. Reviewer skepticism kills applications.

### Use the evaluation criteria as an outline
Every SBIR/STTR is scored on specific criteria (Intellectual Merit, Broader Impacts for NSF; Technical Innovation, Commercial Potential for others). Structure your narrative to address each criterion explicitly.

### Preliminary data is gold
Even basic proof of concept dramatically increases win rates. If you have early data, lead with it.

### Get letters of support
Customer letters expressing interest or commitment (even non-binding) add commercial credibility. One letter from a rancher saying "I would buy this" beats 10 pages of market analysis.

## USDA NIFA SBIR (Agriculture-Specific)

**Priority areas:**
- Precision agriculture and digital tools
- Animal health and welfare technology
- Supply chain traceability
- Climate-smart agriculture
- Rural economic development

**What they want to see:**
- Clear connection to USDA's stated priorities
- Evidence of engagement with agricultural producers
- Path to commercial deployment in ag markets
- Team with agriculture domain knowledge (advisors count)

**Application tips:**
- Use USDA language from their RFA (Request for Applications)
- Reference USDA data (NASS, ERS, APHIS) for problem framing
- Partner with an agricultural university if possible (STTR mechanism)
- Include producer letters of support

## Budget Justification Template

```markdown
## Personnel
- [Name], Principal Investigator (50% effort, 6 months): $X
  Justification: Dr. X will lead all technical development...
- [Name], Software Engineer (100% effort, 6 months): $X
  Justification: Will implement the ML pipeline and mobile app...

## Fringe Benefits
- Applied at institution rate of X% on salaries: $X

## Equipment
- Item: [Description], Cost: $X
  Justification: Required for [specific aim], not currently available...

## Travel
- USDA stakeholder meetings (2 trips × $1,500): $3,000
- Industry conference (1 trip): $2,000
  Justification: Essential for customer validation and dissemination...

## Subcontracts
- [University Partner], Dr. [Name]: $X
  Justification: Provides [specific expertise] not available in-house...

## Other Direct Costs
- Cloud computing (AWS): $X/month × 6 months
- Survey platform (Qualtrics): $X
- Data acquisition: $X

## Indirect Costs (F&A)
- Rate: X% applied to MTDC (modified total direct costs)
```

## Common Rejection Reasons

- **Vague innovation claim** — "Novel AI approach" without specifics
- **No customer evidence** — Market size numbers without interviews or LOIs
- **Weak team** — PI lacks relevant domain expertise
- **Unrealistic timeline** — 6-month Phase I with 5 specific aims
- **Missing commercialization** — Great science, no business plan
- **Budget not justified** — Line items without explanation
- **Didn't follow instructions** — Page limits, font size, required sections
